The Economics of Losing

March 2026 5 min read

At some point, losing stops being a failure of execution.

It becomes something else—something quieter, more deliberate, and, in the right hands, more useful than anyone wants to admit.

Teams do not always lose because they are incompetent. Sometimes they lose because losing creates advantages that competence cannot. Better draft position. Lower expectations. Payroll flexibility. A fan base willing to accept short-term embarrassment in exchange for the promise of a cleaner future.

That promise is the real product. Not wins. Not coherence. Not even visible progress. Just the story that the pain is temporary and the next version of the organization will finally make all of this feel worthwhile.

Front offices rarely describe this as surrender. They call it patience. Discipline. Optionality. A reset. But the language matters less than the incentives. If the cost of losing is manageable and the rewards of bottoming out are tangible, then failure stops functioning like a warning sign and starts functioning like a strategy.

Fans call it rebuilding. Executives call it leverage. Ownership often calls it efficiency.

The uncomfortable part is not that teams lose. It is that the institutions around them have become very good at explaining why losing is, for now, the responsible thing to do.
